The Kildare Longstones

The Kildare Longstones

By Brian Doyle - Archaeoastronomy Ireland

Abstract

This study provides an archaeoastronomic and cultural analysis of eight prominent standing stones (longstones/menhirs) in County Kildare, Ireland, including Punchestown and Forenaghts Great. We examine the multi-functional nature of these Bronze Age monuments, which likely served as calendrical markers, burial sites, and territorial boundaries. The document explores the influence of folklore and the subsequent Christian re-appropriation of the stones, demonstrating their enduring sacral significance in the Irish landscape from prehistory through to the medieval period.

1. Introduction

This article provides an archaeoastronomic examination of eight standing stones in County Kildare, Ireland: Forenaghts Great, Baltracey, Punchestown, Craddockstown West, Great Connell, Kilgowen, Mullaghmast, and the Longstone (Broadleas Commons). The primary goal is to analyze the cultural and symbolic interpretations of these ancient monoliths in relation to celestial phenomena and landscape features.

2. Menhirs: Purpose and Debate

Standing stones, or 'menhirs' (known as *galláns* in southern Ireland), are a common and enigmatic feature of the Irish archaeological landscape. These large, vertically oriented stones were erected in the prehistoric period. While menhirs are widely distributed across Western Europe, they are particularly numerous in Ireland, Great Britain, and Brittany. Despite their prevalence, their precise function is not well understood and remains a subject of ongoing investigation and debate among archaeologists. Most are believed to have been erected from the late Neolithic period to the end of the Iron Age, with a general dating to the Bronze Age or earlier.

The exact purpose of standing stones remains a subject of considerable academic debate, as their intended function is not well understood. Scholars have proposed several theories, each with some supporting evidence. A key area of investigation is the field of 'archaeoastronomy', which examines the alignment and symbolism of ancient monuments in relation to celestial bodies. The theory that standing stones were erected for astronomical or calendrical purposes, aligned with the sun, moon, or stars, is relevant to the study of these longstones. Such alignments, which might have served a symbolic rather than purely predictive function, are seen at other prominent Irish megalithic sites, notably passage tombs. The theory that standing stones were used as 'burial markers' is supported by the findings of burial cists and cremated human remains at the base of stones like Punchestown and Forenaghts Great. Another school of thought suggests they were 'ceremonial or ritual sites', as evidenced by the Forenaghts stone's location within a henge. Finally, it is argued that the stones functioned as 'territorial markers' to denote tribal lands or important meeting points. The proximity of the Punchestown and Craddockstown stones, as well as the suggestion of a wider 5-kilometer circle, lends some credence to this idea. The multidisciplinary nature of the evidence, from archaeology to folklore, indicates that these monoliths may have served multiple, overlapping functions.

3. The Christian Overlap

The study of Kildare's standing stones also reveals a fascinating process of 'cultural continuity'. The Kilgowen stone's Christianized cross is a prime example of this. Rather than destroying the ancient pagan monuments, early Christians often re-appropriated them by carving Christian symbols onto them. This act served to neutralize the pre-Christian spiritual power of the stone while integrating it into the new religious framework. This demonstrates that the standing stones remained places of significance long after their initial erection. This reuse highlights the deep-seated sacrality of these locations, which continued to hold meaning and draw people for purposes of ritual, worship, or commemoration for centuries, even as the form and expression of that reverence changed.

4. A Broader Prehistoric Tapestry

The standing stones of Kildare are not isolated relics; they are embedded within a much wider 'prehistoric tapestry'. The county's landscape features a variety of other ancient sites, including hillforts, henges, stone circles and barrows. Notably, the ceremonial site of 'Dún Aillinne' (Knockaulin), located near Kilcullen, was an important ritual site and the royal seat of the kings of Leinster. This massive hengeform enclosure, larger than Navan Fort in County Armagh, was a site of assembly through the Iron Age and is thought to be where the kings of Leinster were inaugurated.

5. Folklore and Mythology of Irish Standing Stones

The folklore surrounding Irish menhirs is deeply rooted in tales of 'giants and heroes'. The most common motif is the stone-throwing contest, where the immense size and weight of these stones are explained by the superhuman strength of mythological figures. Another belief is that the stones are petrified beings, such as giants or warriors, turned to stone for some transgression. Menhirs were often seen as commemorative monuments, marking the burial places of great heroes or chieftains. Some were believed to possess magical powers. One of the most famous examples of a menhir with a specific myth is the 'Lia Fáil' at the Hill of Tara, which was said to roar when touched by the rightful High King of Ireland.

6. Kildare Specific Sites

Kildare's menhirs have their own unique legends, particularly the Punchestown Standing Stone. According to a story told in the district, the hero 'Fionn Mac Cumhaill' lived on the Hill of Allen and fought a giant for three days. As they could not defeat each other, they agreed to a stone-throwing contest. The giant threw the Craddockstown Stone, but Fionn threw the Punchestown stone further, winning the contest. The Kilgowen stone, meanwhile, has its own unique folklore, with locals believing it marks the grave of a "great man" and even that it "goes to the local stream to drink at night". A Christian cross is carved into the stone, a feature that local stories say was added to vanquish the pagan spirits associated with the monument. This tale illustrates a folkloric clash between old beliefs and new, told through the physical carving on the stone. Beyond these specific tales, the stones are connected to broader cultural beliefs. The 12th-century chronicler Gerald of Wales mentioned a "'Giants' Dance'" on the plains of Kildare, noting that local people believed giants had brought the stones from the "furthest parts of Africa". This ancient belief reflects the human wonder at how such massive stones could have been transported and erected by prehistoric peoples. These stories, while not archaeological fact, are essential to understanding the enduring cultural significance of the stones, which continue to inspire local imagination and are seen as living entities in the landscape.

7. Methodology

7.1 Spatial Contextual Awareness: A Simple Explanation

Spatial Contextual Awareness (SCA) is a framework that explains how location, time, and activity intersect to transform a static location into actionable, meaningful information. It moves beyond simply knowing where you are (a point on a map) to understanding the web of relationships between that location and the people who interact with it.

SCA is a synthesis of three core elements:

  1. 'The Spatial Environment:' The physical location (city, terrain, monument).
  2. 'The Cognitive Agent:' The person or entity completing the task.
  3. 'Contextual Input:' The time of day or the specific activity being performed.

7.2 SCA in a Geographic Context: The Train Station Example

In modern geography, SCA is used to design efficient systems and provide dynamic, real-time guidance.

'Example:' 'Person A' travels to a train station knowing they will meet 'Person B' arriving on a train.

In this scenario:

  • The 'Spatial Environment' is the physical train station and its network of platforms.
  • The 'Cognitive Agent' is Person A, whose task is to intercept Person B.
  • The 'Contextual Input' is the precise arrival 'time' and the specific 'platform' (the activity).

Person A uses the contextual input (the time and platform number) to narrow the vast spatial environment of the station down to one single, critical location. They go to that specific place at that specific time knowing they will have an evident visual phenomenon (the arrival of the train), making the location meaningful for that moment. This is the essence of SCA.

7.3 SCA in Archaeoastronomy: The Sacred Landscape

The same logical concept—choosing a specific location for a predictable, time-based event—is fundamental to landscape or horizon based archaeoastronomy. In this field, SCA is known as recognizing how ancient peoples used their understanding of the landscape's specific features and the celestial cycles to create meaningful relationships between earthly and cosmic phenomena.

It involves viewing archaeological sites, like tombs or henges, not in isolation but as part of a broader system where their location and orientation were deliberately chosen to align with important astronomical events, such as the sunrise or sunset on specific dates 'observable from specific significant locations'. This approach uses their knowledge of space to interpret how ancient cultures perceived their place in the universe through the sky's connection to their sacred geography.

'Example:' 'Person A' (The Ancient Community/Priest) goes to a specific place at a specific time knowing they will have an evident visual cosmic phenomenon, i.e., sun/moon rise/set out of a place or monument they regard as special.

The concept of site-specific alignment linked to sacred geography is perfectly illustrated by the Inca in the Andes. Here, the spatial relationship was established between a viewing point and a distant, sacred natural feature.

The Inca viewed their world through the lens of a 'Sacred Geography', where the landscape itself became the primary representational tool. This involved aligning structures not just with the sky, but with distant, culturally significant mountain peaks (the sacred space) to mark specific celestial events.

For example, near the Inca village of Urubamba, certain structures were intentionally positioned such that when an observer stood at a specific granite boulder in the center of the Huayna Capac's palace, distant stone structures on the summit of 'Cerro Unoraqui' precisely marked the position of the December solstice sunrise.

In this scenario:

  • The 'Spatial Environment/Tool' is the granite viewing boulder and the distant sacred mountain, Cerro Unoraqui.
  • The 'Cognitive Agent' is the Inca observer, tasked with precise calendar keeping, often for agricultural cycles.
  • The 'Contextual Input' is the specific 'time' of the December Solstice sunrise (the celestial event).

The observer goes to this specific place (the boulder) knowing that at that specific time (solstice morning), they will see the sun rise directly behind a pre-existing, special marker (the distant mountain structure), fulfilling the SCA requirement of an observable and repeatable cosmic phenomenon linked to a meaningful location.

7.4 SCA in Archaeoastronomy: The Sacred Landscape in an Irish Context

The exploration of SCA in a prehistoric Irish context seeks to demonstrate that the ancient Irish have been aware that by being present at particular locations at specific times, they will have observable and repeatable astronomic events (phenomena) visible at other prominent locations on the skyline. That the ancient Irish afforded special significance to these locations where they could observe, on divisional days within the solar and lunar cycles, the sun or moon rise from, or set into, what they regarded as sacred mountains or sacred spaces.

8. Individual Archaeological, Landscape and Astronomic Profiles

8.1 The Punchestown Longstone

The 'Punchestown Longstone' is the largest and arguably most striking standing stone in Ireland. Located in a field north of the famous Punchestown Racecourse, this tapering granite monolith was originally found to be nearly 7 meters long and weigh 9 tonnes when it toppled over in 1931. Its dramatic history is significant, as its fall was a catalyst for discovery. In 1934, the stone was re-erected to a slightly shorter, more stable height of almost 6 meters. This process led to the discovery of an empty 'Bronze Age burial cist' at its base. The existence of this cist provides crucial, though inconclusive, evidence for the stone's original function as a burial marker. The stone's modern story, from its accidental fall to its deliberate re-erection, extends its narrative beyond prehistory into the 20th century and highlights how modern events can lead to important archaeological findings.

8.2 The Forenaghts Great Stone

The 'Forenaghts Great Stone' is another impressive monument, standing over 5 meters high and weighing approximately 13 tonnes. This monument is set within a circular banked enclosure with an external ditch, which is recognized not as a ringfort but as a ceremonial 'henge'. Archaeological excavations in 1912 revealed a large rectangular stone cist containing the cremated remains of two adults, believed to be a male and a female. The excavation also found a layer of burnt soil, which led archaeologists to interpret the site as a potential location for deliberate ritual burning following its construction. The explicit evidence of a funerary and ritual context at Forenaghts Great, in contrast to the empty cist at Punchestown, provides strong support for the "burial marker" theory of standing stones. This concrete evidence also differentiates it from other prominent monuments in the county, reinforcing the point that the grouping is not uniform in its purpose or archaeological significance.

8.3 The Baltracey Stone

Situated approx halfway between the Forenaghts Great and Punchestown stones, the Baltracey menhir is a fine example of a standing stone, measuring some 3.5 meters high. Located at the eastern end of a low ridge, with long, gentle slopes falling away immediately to the North, East and South, and with level pasture running off to the West. A tall, granite pillar, the upper portion of which has been roughly worked into the shape of a rude cross. The stone appears to be a granite conglomerate, with some white quartz inclusions visible under the southern arm of the cross. The stone is roughly shaped, being a rounded-rectangular on a N-S axis at the base (Wth 0.95m; T 0.7m) before narrowing to an almost square section above (Wth 0.45m; T 0.4m), with stubby arms (Wth 0.6m) near the top. Several large, rough blocks at the base, not all of them earthfast, may have been an attempt at creating a rough plinth.

8.4 The Craddockstown West Stone: A Companion to Punchestown

The 'Craddockstown West stone' is located just 800 meters southwest of the Punchestown monolith, near the entrance to the racecourse. Standing at 4.3 meters high, it is a significant monument in its own right. Its close proximity to Punchestown supports the theory that the stones in this vicinity form a larger, interconnected ceremonial landscape. Some sources suggest this group of stones forms a "rough circle of approximately five kilometres in diameter" , suggesting they functioned not as isolated markers but as components of a larger system, perhaps for territorial demarcation or communal ritual. The relationship between these two stones is an important element in understanding the spatial organization of the prehistoric landscape in this part of Kildare.

8.5 The Mullaghmast Standing Stone

The 'Mullaghmast Standing Stone' (KD036-017001-) is a distinct Bronze Age monolith that was moved from its original location on the opposite side of the road, approximately 10 meters, to the east side of a road on a low ridge. This slightly tapering pillar of granite stands 1.8 meters high with a sub-rectangular cross-section. It is notable for 'three grooves descending from the top' (likely natural) that link it stylistically to other grooved standing stones found in Carlow and Wicklow. This prehistoric standing stone should not be confused with the later, iron age or early medieval ceremonial stone also associated with Mullaghmast Hill.

8.6 The Longstone (Broadleas Commons)

The 'Longstone (Broadleas Commons)' is located on a low glacial ridge. A long slender, rounded, granite pillar, currently lying 'prostrate' (L 4.1m; Wth. 0.9m; T 0.65m). According to historical records from the 1839 Ordnance Survey Letters (OSL), the stone ‘stood in a small earthen moat, but now lies on the ground, having been thrown down about 2 or 3 years ago.’ Two small crosses on the upper surface were probably incised sometime after the stone was thrown down. A unique physical characteristic is that one quarter of the stone (L 1.6m) is unsupported and overhangs (H 0.4m) the ground below.

8.7 The Kilgowen Standing Stone

The 'Kilgowen Standing Stone' is located towards the north end of a low, partially quarried-away esker. It is a tall (H. 2.3m), thin tapering granite stone, with a base measuring 0.61m long by 0.46m wide, tapering towards the top (0.46m long by 0.3m wide). A later 'cross is incised' on its southwest face. Local folklore further contributes to this sense of a living monument, with stories that it marks the grave of a "great man" and even that it "goes to the local stream to drink at night". While the cross may be associated with burials (KD032-012002-) discovered immediately to the north in 1987, historical sources indicate that the cross was incised by one of two local priests, Father Lawlor or Father Cummins, who lived near the crossroads 100 years or more before 1936. The stone is situated on what may be an artificial mound and is subject to a preservation order (PO no. 7/1973) made under the National Monuments Acts 1930 to 2014, reflecting its archaeological significance.. This 'Christian overlay' provides physical evidence of syncretism, where pagan monuments were often re-appropriated for Christian use, indicating a continuity of sacrality for the site.

8.8 The Great Connell Standing Stone

The 'Great Connell Standing Stone' is a monolith located near the highest point of a gravel ridge. A tapering granite stone with a roughly rectangular cross-section (H. 1.9m; L 0.4m N-S; Wth 0.3m tapering to L 0.2m; Wth 0.14m at the top). There are two depressions (possibly mortices cut for cross arms) on opposing, originally N and S, faces. The stone was removed in 1979 and re-erected at Baroda stud in Greatconnell townland, but was moved again subsequently. Burials (KD023-071----) have been discovered in this general area since the late-1940’s.

9.2 Target Sites

Dún Ailinne

Dún Ailinne, an ancient ceremonial site in County Kildare, Ireland, functioned as a major 'Iron Age' royal and inaugural center, but its structural and ritual origins likely extend back to the 'Neolithic' and 'Early Bronze Age' periods.


Iron Age Ceremonial Role

The site's primary activity phase, the Iron Age (ca. 400 BCE – 400 CE), saw the construction of a large circular enclosure (about 13 ha) ringed by a symbolic, internal ditch—a 'henge'. Archaeological investigations revealed sophisticated internal structures, including several phases of 'circular timber rings' (or upright posts), one with a distinct figure-eight layout. Dún Ailinne served as a major ritual hub for the Kings of Leinster, paralleling sites like Tara and Navan Fort. The activity here was short-term and ceremonial, occurring mainly during the spring/summer months and involving extensive feasting, as evidenced by large quantities of cooked animal bone. Artifacts like a 'La Tène style sword' and Roman bronze fibulae confirm intense Iron Age occupation.


Neolithic/Early Bronze Age Mound and Standing Stone

Despite its Iron Age prominence, the earliest indications of use at Dún Ailinne are from the 'Neolithic period'. The summit features a low, symmetrical mound, later confirmed to be an 'artificial prehistoric mound' that was heavily reduced over time. Excavation of this feature revealed a layer of soil containing both 'Neolithic and Iron Age material', suggesting the Iron Age rituals superseded an earlier occupation.


Crucially, there is strong evidence for an early monument associated with a stone. The site's name, *Alend* (Ailinne), is linked by the medieval *Dindsenchas* tradition to an ''Ail' (Stone)' placed inside the enclosure by the hero Buirech. Archaeologist Estyn Evans suggested that a scattering of stone debris and the large 'boulders' still visible on the summit—which John O'Donovan recorded in 1837—may be the 'remains of a round Neolithic or Early Bronze Age cairn'. This suggests the ceremonial focus of Dún Ailinne began with a stone monument or burial mound that predated and possibly informed its later use as a great Iron Age sanctuary.



Brewel Hill


The Brewel West complex is a major Ceremonial Enclosure situated on the summit of Brewel Hill (c. 221m) in County Kildare, forming part of a wider prehistoric landscape that includes nearby ringbarrows and a possible cursus. The monument features a large circular earthwork with an overall diameter of approximately 75m and an internal diameter of c. 39m. It consists of a low earthen bank and a shallow outer fosse (ditch). While the external fosse is noted as unusual for a typical henge, it doesn't necessarily disassociate the site from the 'henge' tradition. A gap in the bank at the NNE may represent the original entrance to this extensive earthwork.


Within the main enclosure, there are structural elements that suggest multiple phases of use, including the remnants of a smaller, non-concentric internal enclosure (estimated original diameter c. 26m). Situated inside this smaller circuit is the feature known as 'The Piper's Stones,' which may represent the surviving southern portion of a former stone circle (estimated original diameter c. 16m). This arc consists of four large, unworked boulders, including two granite stones, a red conglomerate (puddingstone), and a white quartz stone locally called the "Piper's Chair." The non-concentric alignment of the smaller internal bank suggests it may be chronologically associated with these stones, with the large outer earthwork being a later addition.


Finally, the entire Brewel Hill complex includes evidence of a possible cursus monument—a long, linear prehistoric earthwork—located to the ESE. This cursus appears on satellite images as cropmarks defining an area approximately 130m long (NW-SE). The linear monument aligns with Keadeen Mountain and Brusselstown Ring to the southeast, connecting the Brewel site into a broader network of significant regional prehistoric structures. It is very important to note that from the Brewel Hill location, in the Neolithic and Early Bronze Age, the sun at the winter solstice could be observed to set directly into the cursus-associated cairn on the summit of Keadeen Mountain.



The Hill of Allen


The Hill of Allen (Cnoc Alúine) is a volcanically formed, historically and mythologically significant site in County Kildare, prominently known in Irish Mythology as the legendary seat and fortress of the hunter-warrior Fionn mac Cumhaill and his army, the Fianna. The hill gives its name to the surrounding Bog of Allen, and local legends claim that Fionn used the flatlands below as training grounds for his warriors. A specific and famous tradition ties the hill directly to the Long Stone at Punchestown (reputed to be the tallest standing stone in Ireland): the stone is said to be the projectile Fionn threw from the Hill of Allen during a stone-throwing contest with a rival giant, which he won.


The primary archaeological monument remaining on the hill is a low, flat-topped, grass-covered sub-circular mound known as Suidhe Finn or Finn's Chair (base diameters c. 43m E-W by 39m N-S), which occupies the highest point. The mound's composition appears to be a fawn-coloured, boulder clay-like material. Modern features include the 19th-century Aylmer Tower standing on the mound. Earlier accounts suggest that a cist-vein (a stone burial box) was unearthed during the digging of the Tower's foundations, providing evidence of prehistoric funerary use for the hill, a usage entirely separate from its later mythological associations with Fionn and the Fianna. Today, the hill's profile has been significantly altered by extensive quarrying, leaving a sheer cliff edge close to the summit.


The site at Caureen is a prominently located earthen Mound on a hill summit in County Kildare, offering panoramic views in all directions. This low, circular, grass-covered mound measures approximately 17m in diameter with a height ranging from 1.5m to 2m. Its most notable feature is a large central depression (average 3m diameter), which may indicate either modern disturbance or the collapse of a potential burial chamber. Archaeological classification suggests it is a possible passage tomb (listed as Kd 1 by Herity), a type of Neolithic monument. Evidence supporting its structure as a prehistoric tomb includes a small earthfast boulder at the base on the north side, which may be a remnant kerbstone, with a second potential kerbstone displaced to the west.


Locally, the hill is known as Caureen Hill and is associated with folklore concerning a hidden treasure. The mound itself is locally referred to as a "moat." According to legend, a man named Spooner—a robber who targeted local gentlemen—concealed his valuables in a hidden location on the hill called "Spooner's Cave." Before he could retrieve the hoard, his secret was exposed, forcing him to flee to America in a barrel without disclosing the cave's location. This romantic legend, which speaks of a great hidden treasure, contrasts with the archaeological interpretation of the site as an ancient burial monument.



Seefin Passage Tomb


Situated on the summit of Seefin Mountain, the monument is a circular cairn (diameter 24–6m; height 3m) defined by a contiguous kerb of large boulders, which is partially removed at the east and west. This cairn covers a narrow lintelled passage (length 11m) that is orientated NE-SW and opens into a rectangular corbelled chamber (dimensions 4m x 1.5m). Off this main chamber are five recesses (two on each side and one at the end), though only the end and adjoining recesses are currently visible, as the others are buried under the collapsed cairn material. Notably, there are two sidestones within the passage that are decorated with passage tomb art. Despite being excavated in 1931, the monument yielded no evidence of burials or finds. Once again, there is a connection to Fionn, as the Irish name for the mountain is Suí Finn, which means "Seat of Fionn," referring to the legendary leader of the Fianna warrior hunters in the Fenian Cycle of Irish mythology.



Seefingan Passage Tomb


Situated on the summit of Seefingan Mountain, this monument is a circular cairn measuring 20m in diameter and 3m in height. Local accounts claim the cairn contains a 'cave' with a 'tunnel' leading into it. While no kerb or internal structures are currently visible, this site has been suggested by archaeologists to be a passage tomb.



Saggart Hill Passage Tomb (Slievethoul)


The summit of Saggart Hill (also known as Slieve Thoul, derived from the Irish *Sliabh Toll*, meaning ‘The Mountain of the Hollow’) is crowned by two Neolithic passage tombs dating to around 3,500–3,300 BC. These tombs originally consisted of great mounds of stone and earth covering a stone-lined passageway leading to a burial chamber. One of these monuments, situated in a clearing, is a flat-topped cairn (diameter 24m; height 1m) that may represent the denuded remains of a passage grave, lying approximately 300m ESE of the second tomb. Historical accounts from the 1830s noted that both cairns were open, describing the larger one as having been recently disturbed, with a "large grave" covered by a massive flagstone that was broken and removed, though its supporting stones remained.



9. Conclusion

The Kildare Longstones represent far more than isolated remnants of the past; they constitute an interconnected ceremonial and cultural landscape. From the winter solstice alignments suggested by archaeoastronomy to the Christian crosses carved onto their faces, these monoliths stand as tangible records of Irish belief systems and cultural transitions. Their survival, coupled with the rich layers of associated mythology and the concrete evidence of ritual use and burial, underscores their profound, multi-faceted importance to the ancient inhabitants of Leinster, demanding continued interdisciplinary study.
