I see you - The relationship between Knowth and Dowth

By Brian Doyle - Archaeoastronomy Ireland.

Abstract

This article presents an analysis of the astronomical function of the Knowth passage tomb. Employing remote Geographic Information System (GIS) methodology the true orientation of the eastern passage has been determined with a high degree of accuracy. The analysis reveals a line of symmetry in the tomb's entrance stones that precisely aligns the eastern passage with the Dowth passage tomb. This alignment, achieved through a 40-meter passage, the longest in Western Europe, demonstrates a remarkable level of engineering sophistication. Unlike Newgrange's reliance on a separate lightbox, Knowth's eastern passage was engineered as a single, precise instrument. The findings conclude that the eastern passage of Knowth was specifically designed to capture the first rays of a celestial body rising from the Dowth mound on the horizon, establishing a deliberate and highly sophisticated astronomical relationship between the two monuments.

I. Introduction: Knowth in the Context of Brú na Bóinne

1.1. Brú na Bóinne: The Archaeological Ensemble of the Boyne Valley

The UNESCO World Heritage Site of Brú na Bóinne, or the Archaeological Ensemble of the Bend of the Boyne, stands as Ireland’s richest archaeological landscape, situated in a meander of the River Boyne in County Meath. The site is globally renowned for its spectacular prehistoric passage tombs, which include the principal monuments of Newgrange, Dowth, and Knowth. Constructed around 3200 BC during the Neolithic period, these colossal structures are a testament to the sophistication of their builders, predating the main pyramids at Giza by approximately 400 years and the main trilithon phase of Stonehenge by 500 to 700 years. The monuments are not isolated features but are part of a larger necropolis or cemetery that also includes henges, burial mounds, and standing stones.

1.2. Knowth's Position within the Complex

While Newgrange often receives the most public attention, Knowth is, in many respects, the most significant monument within the Brú na Bóinne complex. It holds the distinction of being the largest passage tomb, with a massive main mound reaching about 12 meters in height and measuring between 67 and 95 meters in diameter, covering roughly 1 hectare (2.5 acres). Unlike Newgrange, which has a single passage, Knowth is uniquely characterized by its two independent passages, placed along an east-west line. A continuous ring of 127 kerbstones, of which 124 remain, encircles the base of the great mound. The main mound is also surrounded by a series of 17 or 18 smaller satellite tombs, several of which are as large as 20 meters in diameter and are believed to predate the central cairn. The site's most remarkable feature, however, is its unparalleled concentration of prehistoric art. Knowth is home to the largest assemblage of megalithic art in Europe, boasting over 400 engraved stones. This collection represents more than a third of the total known examples of such art in all of Western Europe, a fact that firmly establishes Knowth as a global center of Neolithic artistic expression.

II. History and Mythology of Knowth

2.1. The Neolithic Foundation (c. 3200 BC)

Knowth was built during the Neolithic or New Stone Age by a wealthy farming community that thrived on the fertile lands of the Boyne Valley. The construction of the central mound and its surrounding satellite tombs demonstrates a high degree of social organization and technical skill. Builders transported local stones, or "pebbles," for the main cairn from nearby river terraces , while sourcing larger structural slabs and decorative stones from quarries as far as 50 kilometers away. This was an enormous expenditure of labor and resources, estimated to have taken at least 30 years to complete, which points to a powerful belief in the hereafter and a cohesive, purpose-driven society. The site’s initial function was that of a passage tomb, a place of burial and ritual. The cremated remains of the dead were placed on basin stones within the chambers. The relatively small number of individuals buried here suggests that the monument’s value was more ceremonial than strictly funerary, serving as an important cultural and spiritual center for the community. Evidence from radiocarbon dating indicates that the burial activity at Knowth may have been concentrated in a "short burst" between 3200 and 2900 BC, rather than serving as an ossuary for successive generations. Interestingly, archaeological findings reveal that some of the satellite mounds predate the colossal central cairn, indicating a multi-phase construction process over a period of at least three centuries.

2.2. Mythology and Folklore

The landscape of Brú na Bóinne is deeply embedded in Irish mythology, and Knowth is no exception. In ancient Irish folklore, Knowth is known as Cnoc Buí, the Hill of Buí. Buí was the sovereignty goddess and consort of Lugh of the Long Arm, a prominent figure in the Irish pantheon. Mythological accounts state that she was buried within the great mound, and the pair of standing stones at the western entrance may symbolically represent Lugh and Buí.A prevailing mythological interpretation suggests that each of the three great mounds in the Boyne Valley holds a symbolic role in a life cycle: Newgrange represents Birth, Knowth represents Marriage, and Dowth represents Death. This symbolic journey from one mound to the next represents a ritualistic path into the underworld and a safe return, emphasizing the passage tombs as conduits between the living and the dead. Knowth’s dual passages may be a physical manifestation of this symbolic role, representing a union or coming together, akin to a marriage, a place of connection and convergence. Other traditions associate the "cave of Cnogba" with being an entrance to the Otherworld.

2.3. The Post-Neolithic

The history of Knowth extends far beyond its initial purpose as a Neolithic tomb, offering a rare glimpse into the continuous cultural re-appropriation of a single site over millennia. After its primary period of use, the mound gradually fell into disrepair, but the area remained a focal point for ritual activity during the Bronze Age, as evidenced by a grooved ware timber circle and a large number of votive offerings found near the eastern passage entrance.In the late Iron Age and early Christian period, around the time of Christ, the site was transformed into a hill fort and became a habitation site for the first time. Two large ditches were dug around the mound, and souterrains were added. It was at this time that the ancient passages seem to have been rediscovered, with early Christian graffiti and four names carved in ogham script on the stones of the eastern chamber, forging a direct physical link between the old and new inhabitants. Knowth rose to political prominence as the capital of the Kingdom of Northern Brega. Archaeological findings from this period are remarkably rich, with material from the tenth to thirteenth centuries comparable to what has been recovered from Viking-era Dublin, suggesting a vibrant period of trade and influence.The site’s strategic importance continued, albeit briefly, into the Norman period, when it was used as a motte in the twelfth century. Following the Norman invasion, the site passed into the hands of the Cistercian monks of Mellifont Abbey, who used it as a grange or farm, building stone walls and structures on top of the ancient mound. This agricultural use continued until 1939, when the site was acquired by the state, marking the beginning of its modern role as a national monument and a subject of scientific inquiry. The continuous occupation and transformation of the site from a sacred burial ground to a royal seat and then a farm illustrate its enduring cultural and strategic significance to successive generations.

III. The Unearthing of a Monument

3.1. Early Investigations

The modern history of Knowth begins with its first scholarly mention by Sir Thomas Molyneux around 1713. However, the early approach to such sites was often driven by curiosity rather than rigorous methodology. This is exemplified by the "unprofessional excavation" at the nearby Dowth mound in 1847, which severely damaged the monument. A more systematic, but still limited, investigation was carried out by R. A. S. Macalister in 1941. He dug a trench in an attempt to find the entrance to the mound and, in the process, exposed 58 kerbstones, 48 of which were engraved. Macallister’s work, while not a full-scale excavation, was the first to document the monument's extensive artistic legacy.

3.2. The Work of Professor George Eogan

The systematic and thorough archaeological investigation of Knowth began in 1962, led by Professor George Eogan of University College Dublin. Eogan, who had previously worked on a passage tomb at Fourknocks, was tasked with investigating the potential of the Knowth site. His decades-long project, which lasted over 40 years, became a foundational model for modern archaeological inquiry, shifting the focus from spectacular finds to a comprehensive understanding of a site's entire history.The excavations yielded several dramatic breakthroughs. In 1967, Eogan’s team discovered a small hole on the western side of the mound that led to a chamber at the end of a 34-meter passage. A year later, on August 1, 1968, an even more significant discovery was made: the eastern passage and its chamber. Eogan himself recounted the excitement of crawling into the void with a flashlight, revealing the impressive, well-constructed chamber that was over 20 feet high. These discoveries revealed that Knowth was not only the largest passage tomb in the Brú na Bóinne complex but also uniquely contained two independent passages.Beyond the passages, Eogan’s team uncovered 18 smaller satellite tombs, revealing the full extent of the complex. The excavations also yielded a wealth of artifacts, including a beautifully engraved flint macehead found in the eastern tomb in 1982. This ceremonial object is considered one of the finest works of art to have survived from Neolithic Europe. Eogan's meticulous, multi-volume publications on the site have transformed our understanding of Knowth from a mere mound into a "multi-period and multi-cultural site" with a rich and complex chronological sequence.

IV. Site Description

4.1. The Overall Site Layout and Scale

The Knowth complex is an imposing and intricate archaeological landscape. The centerpiece is the great mound, a massive structure about 12 meters high and 67 to 95 meters in diameter, which covers an acre of ground. The main cairn is surrounded by a ring of 17 or 18 smaller satellite mounds. Most of these smaller mounds are also passage tombs, and some are as large as 20 meters in diameter, with archaeological evidence suggesting they predate the main mound. A reconstruction of a grooved ware timber circle stands near the entrance to the eastern passage, providing a visual representation of the site’s later ritual use.

4.2. The Eastern Passage and Chamber

The eastern passage is a marvel of Neolithic engineering and is the longest megalithic passage in Western Europe, stretching 40 meters. It culminates in a cruciform chamber, a layout similar to the chamber at Newgrange, with a central space and three side recesses. The chamber is topped by a corbelled roof, a series of overlapping stones that form a high, conical vault, which has remained intact for over 5,000 years.This chamber yielded some of the most spectacular artifacts found at Knowth. A large basin stone, often referred to as the "Dagdas cauldron" was found in the right-hand recess. One of the most significant finds was a ceremonial macehead, recovered from beneath the chamber in 1982. Carved from a hard, creamy-grey flint, this small object is exquisitely decorated on each of its six sides with spirals, whorls, and diamond patterns. Some researchers suggest the designs and shape of the macehead are meant to represent a human face, with the shaft hole serving as the mouth. This object is considered one of the finest works of art from Neolithic Europe and is now displayed at the National Museum of Ireland.

4.3. The Western Passage and Chamber

Located on the opposite side of the mound, the western passage is 34 meters in length. It leads to an "undifferentiated" chamber, meaning the passage simply widens to form the burial chamber, without the distinct cruciform shape of the eastern tomb. A basin stone was also present in this chamber, but at some point in the monument's later history, an attempt was made to remove it. The stone became stuck in the passage because it was wider than the space itself, where it remains to this day. The western passage also contained a small stone phallus, another artifact that provides a glimpse into the ritualistic life of the Neolithic builders.

4.4. The Megalithic Art of Knowth

Knowth's most celebrated feature is its vast collection of megalithic art, which represents the largest assemblage of its kind in Western Europe. A total of 261 stones are decorated, accounting for approximately 45% of all engraved megalithic stones in Ireland. A continuous ring of 127 kerbstones encircles the base of the mound, and of the 124 that survive, a remarkable 90 are engraved with intricate patterns.The art is primarily abstract and geometrical, created using chisels and punch tools.

Common motifs include spirals, lozenges, zigzags, concentric circles, and serpentine forms. However, Knowth’s art also includes unique images, such as crescent shapes and what is considered to be the oldest known illustration of the moon in history.A significant portion of this artwork is of a type known as "hidden art," where carvings were placed on the backs of stones that would be concealed within the mound.

The intentional concealment of this art has led to several theories about its purpose. One hypothesis is that the art was not intended for public viewing but had a private or structural ritualistic purpose. An alternative theory suggests that the builders simply recycled stones from an older monument, reusing the uncarved side for the new construction. This practice of using stones sourced from distant quarries, a common feature of Brú na Bóinne monuments, supports the idea that material was carefully selected and perhaps even repurposed.The architectural differences and the distinct nature of the artifacts found in the two passages suggest that they may have served different functions. The elaborate, cruciform eastern chamber, with its spectacular macehead and basin, appears to have been the primary ceremonial space. The western passage, with its simpler design and the displaced basin stone, may have had a different, perhaps less revered, role. These nuances highlight the complexity of Knowth's design, suggesting that its purpose was not monolithic but rather a dynamic, multi-functional sacred space.

V. Celestial Observatory: The Astronomical Debates of Knowth

5.1. Solar vs. Lunar: A Foundational Debate

Knowth's astronomical purpose has been a subject of intense academic debate, and its story stands in stark contrast to the well-known and widely accepted solar alignment of Newgrange. The long-held initial theory, popularized by researchers such as Martin Brennan and George Eogan, proposed that Knowth's two passages were aligned with the sunrise and sunset of the spring and autumn equinoxes.

This theory was based on the east-west orientation of the passages and the symbolic importance of the equinoxes, which would have marked the beginning of the growing season and the time of harvest for the Neolithic farming community.However, this theory has been largely debunked by modern research. The entrances to the passages were altered by later settlers, making a precise alignment impossible to verify today. Furthermore, the passages themselves are not aligned to a precise east-west axis, but rather at an angle that would have captured light several days before or after the equinox.

5.2. The Lunar Hypothesis

The prevailing hypothesis now suggests that Knowth’s primary astronomical function was lunar. The evidence supporting this theory is limited but compelling and suggests a more profound understanding of celestial mechanics than previously assumed. Lunar hypothesis are largely based on the kerbstone artwork. Supposed key pieces of evidence for the lunar theory include Orthostat 47, a carving on a vertical stone at the end of the eastern passage, has been 'identified' by planetary cartographer Philip J. Stooke as a map of the lunar maria, or "seas".

This proposed detailed depiction of the moon's surface as seen with the naked eye and is considered the by some as the oldest known lunar map in history. The Kerbstones: The vast majority of the carved stones at Knowth are believed to bear lunar symbols and calculations. Kerbstone 5, for instance, has been interpreted as a depiction of the phases of a total solar eclipse, an event that is predictable only with a sophisticated understanding of the lunar cycle. Kerbstone 30 features "Opposed Cs," which may represent the first and last days of the lunar month. The presence of 18 satellite mounds surrounding the main cairn has been linked to the 18.6-year lunar cycle, a complex celestial pattern that would have required meticulous, long-term observation to track. This shift in scholarly opinion from a simple solar alignment to a complex lunar hypothesis would require a profound intellectual sophistication of the Neolithic people. The solar cycle is a predictable, 365-day rhythm, but the lunar cycle is far more complex to track and requires a much higher level of astronomical knowledge to predict events like eclipses. The juxtaposition of a solar-aligned monument (Newgrange) with a potential lunar-focused one (Knowth) suggests that the Brú na Bóinne complex was not a series of similar tombs but a holistic astronomical observatory, a dual calendar system for tracking both the solar year and the lunar month and lunar cycles. These theories elevate the Neolithic builders from mere sun-worshippers to advanced scientists who were capable of mapping and predicting the most complex celestial movements visible to the naked eye.

VI. Inaccuracy

To make any definitive observations or claims in the field of archaeoastronomy, there is a reliance on accurate and precise data. Central to this is the correct measurement of a feature's orientation or azimuth. For the monuments of Brú na Bóinne, a foundational body of data comes from the work of M.J. O'Kelly, George Eogan and their respective teams. However, all plans for all of the three main passage tombs have been found to contain inaccuracies. Where these plans are perfectly suitable for understanding the physical layout and form of the tomb, these issues render the plans totally unsuitable for astronomical analysis. A direct result of the incorrect orientation of the Knowth passages led to the proposed (and incorrect) hypothesis of alignment on the equinox rise and set. In 'The megalithic art of the passage tombs at Knowth' version 7, these issues are explained and more importantly corrected!

VII. Tomb Design Analysis

7.1. Sophisticated Techniques

An examination of specific architectural and design elements common to both the Newgrange and Knowth passage tombs suggests that the builders used sophisticated planning and surveying techniques. These elements also indicate each tomb's primary orientation.

7.2. Newgrange Vertically Grooved Stones

Kerbstone 52

Kerbstone 52 Newgrange is a stone located to the rear of the passage tomb. It is the stone furthest removed from the entrance stone. This highly decorated stone is characterised by a deeply incised vertical groove which seperates distinct panels of artwork.

Entrance Stone

The entrance stone to Newgrange, famous for it's decorative curvilinear and spirals based artwork, also features a distinctive deeply incised vertical groove.

7.3. Newgrange Symmetry and Orientation

Using the overhead plan for newgrange we can draw a line of symmetry between these vertically grooved stones. This line describes the tomb's longest axis. The line intersects key architectural and design features. The line intersects the chamber triple spiral motif, passes directly through the passage, next bisecting the decorated panels of the roofbox. This line precisely marks the orientation of the tomb on the winter solstice sunrise. This suggests planning and significant surveying skills have been employed by the builders of Newgrange.

7.4. Knowth Vertically Grooved Stones

Kerbstone 74

The entrance to the western passage is fronted by Kerbstone 74. The design might be described as consisting of a panel representing a type of squared spiral or series of concentric lines. This continous pattern is divided by deep vertical groove down the center of the stone.

Kerbstone 11

The entrance to the eastern passage is fronted by Kerbstone 11. The design might be described as consisting of 2 panels of squared spirals or series of concentric lines. These panels are divided by a deep vertical groove down the center of the stone.

7.5. Knowth Symmetry and Orientation

The entrance stones to the Knowth passages are analogous with the Newgrange vertically grooved stones. Completing the same exercise with the vertically grooved entrance stones for Knowth we can make the following observations. Working from west to east, the line of symmetry exactly intersects the southmost corner of the undifferentiated chamber of Knowth's western passage. It next exactly bisects the rear stone of the recess of the eastern chamber (Orthostat 47), before exactly defining the centerline of the eastern passage. Here again we must conclude that this has been planned and executed with the same significant surveying skills in evidence. We also might assess that the pre-eminent orientation of Knowth is that of the eastern passage, as the line of symmetry is directed along this azimuth.

VIII. Horizon Analysis

8.1. Getting a view

Completing an in in situ analsysis of the Dowth passage tomb would be a extremely difficult if not impossible task. The view from the eastern passage is significantly restricted, hindering any astronomical or topographical analysis. This obstruction is due to several key factors:

Damage and Alterations:
The entrance to the eastern passage has suffered considerable damage over time and has been altered from its original state. These changes have likely compromised the passage's initial sightlines.

Modern Infrastructure:
The introduction of modern infrastructure, specifically designed to facilitate public access, has inadvertently created new obstructions. Features such as walkways, railings, and other structural additions block the natural view from within the passage.

Environmental Obstructions:
The surrounding environment further compounds the issue. Nearby trees and buildings completely obscure any view toward the horizon, making it impossible to observe celestial events from this specific vantage point.

8.2. Geographic Information Systems

Our initial task is to georeference the Knowth plan and overlay it onto a modern map. This remote process provides a significant advantage by offering a clear overhead view of the internal passages while simultaneously placing them within their broader, surrounding environment. This allows for a comprehensive analysis of the site’s layout and its relationship to the surrounding landscape, which is essential for understanding the builders’ intent. Using GIS mapping and azimuth measurement tools, we can also, with extreme accuracy, recreate the tomb's line of symmetry. This analysis reveals an angle of 84° (83.8° in the WGS 84 coordinate system)

To overcome the issues of an obstructed view, we can create a viewshed horizon for the site. This is a 360-degree calculation of all visible horizon points. For this analysis, we used a digital terrain model (DTM) that combines high-resolution LiDAR data for local horizons with 1 arc-second SRTM data for more distant ones. A Digital Terrain Model (DTM) is a digital representation of a bare-earth surface. It has been processed to remove all features above the ground, such as trees and buildings. Using a DTM in our analysis allows us to accurately calculate a viewshed horizon as we suspect it would have appeared in prehistory, before these modern obstructions were present. The viewshed horizon, indicated by the blue line in the image, reveals a significant observation: the Dowth passage tomb mound is clearly visible on the horizon in a generally easterly direction. This immediate and easily recognized observation highlights the intervisibility between these two major passage tombs, which is crucial for understanding the broader prehistoric landscape and the intentional placement of these monuments.

Based on the analysis, we can make a key observation: the direction toward the Dowth passage tomb appears to be generally consistent with the line of symmetry of Knowth's eastern passage. To confirm this, we extend the original line of symmetry directly out to the horizon. In the accompanying image, this line has not been angle-adjusted in any way, it has simply been extended. Our analysis shows that this line of symmetry is exactly directed at the center point of the Dowth passage tomb mound. In other words, the eastern passage of Knowth is precisely directed at the Dowth passage tomb on the horizon, suggesting a deliberate and precise relationship between the two monumental sites.

IX. Light Penetration

9.1. Sideview

The next task is to determine if it was possible for any light to penetrate the eastern passage and reach the chamber's rear recess. For this analysis, the side-view plan of the eastern passage is used to measure all possible angles where light could enter. This plan is crucial for examining the tomb's internal geometry and the vertical alignment of its roof, floor, and entrance stone. A consideration in this analysis is a likely displaced passage capstone near the entrance. However, by measuring all possible entry angles, a definite 'window of opportunity' for light to enter the passage was determined. This window is a narrow range of elevations, specifically between approximately 0.75° and 1.0° above the horizon. This finding is significant because it suggests the builders deliberately designed the passage to allow light to enter only under very specific conditions.

9.2. Topview

While there is evident disturbance to the orthostats close to the passage entrance, where we examine the passage in the horizontal plane, a direct light path is nevertheless available. By analyzing the angles from an overhead perspective, it was determined that this path constitutes a narrow opening of approximately 0.4° in width.

IX. Astronomy

9.1. Modelling

Having determined that light entry within the eastern passage has been possible, the analysis now moves to a three-dimensional model of the landscape. By creating a modeled landscape for the Knowth location using LiDAR and SRTM data, the precise view to the horizon can be shown. Importing this modelled landscape into astronomy software allows us to introduce the fourth dimension of time.

Using a neolithic timeframe:
Dowth Eastern Passage Sun rise @ 83.8° 8 days before/after equinox.
Dowth Western Passage Sun set @ 258° 17/18 days before/after equinox (sun rising at 103°).

These azimuth values do not demonstrate any correlation with any known solar/lunar patterns beyond predictable specific solar rises and sets. Moon rises and sets (including full moon rises/sets) are certainly demonstrable, but are they predicatable ?

9.2. Visualisation

Using the modelled landscape typical sun or moon rises out of the Dowth mound can be demonstrated. A suggested lightbox based on the analysis of the site plans can also be indicated. The only light that could ever penetrate through to the rear recess of the eastern passage of Knowth would have been the very first rays of the sun, the moon, or a planet rising from the Dowth passage tomb on the horizon.


Hypothesised Lighting Effect.

Based on the visual experience of light entry at Newgrange we can suggest the likely lighting effect within the Knowth eastern passage chamber. As first light enters it will immediately strike the center of the end recess back stone (Orthostat 47). The light beam will quickly move downwards forming a spear of light which then progressively exits the chamber.

9.2. Directionality

The examination of the Dowth passage tomb has been demonstrated that passage tombs have been present directly in front of both the north and south passages. Here we have demonstrated the Dowth passage tomb to be directly in front of Knowths eastern passage. It is worth noting that Knowth passage tomb Site 7 (ME019-030007-) is located some 160 meters to the southwest, directly in front of the western passage.

Conclusion

This analysis of the eastern passage of Knowth leads to a definitive conclusion: it was specifically designed to capture light from a celestial body rising out of the Dowth passage tomb on the horizon. The passage itself, at 40 meters, is a testament to the builders' advanced skills, creating an incredibly narrow horizontal and vertical window for light. Unlike the Newgrange passage tomb which relies on a separate lightbox, Knowth's builders engineered the passage to be the sole instrument for this precise alignment. The combined evidence leaves no doubt that the eastern passage of Knowth was not merely a tomb, but a sophisticated astronomical instrument.
