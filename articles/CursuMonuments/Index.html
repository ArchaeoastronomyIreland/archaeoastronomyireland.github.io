Abstract: The Premise

The ancient Irish afforded special significance to locations where they could observe, on divisional days within the solar and lunar cycles, the sun or moon rise from, or set into, what they regarded as sacred mountains.

In this presentation, I describe and chart the inherent astronomic interrelationships, which in neolithic times, existed between locations and precincts which have an associated cursus monument.

1. Methodology: Seeing Through Neolithic Eyes

The observations described in this presentation have been determined using a combination of GIS (Geographic Information System) derived values, computer generated landscapes, and astronomy software.

This enables us to get a view of the surrounding landscape for the sites of interest and accurately locate other sites of interest on the visible horizon. It allows us to recreate the state of the cosmos for the relevant time period, and most importantly in the context of this presentation, identify the positions on the horizon where the sun and moon could be observed to rise and set on divisional days within the solar and lunar cycles.

2. Cursus Locations Intervisibility Network

In this section, the cursus locations are plotted as an intervisibility network. Each location is represented by a node. Interconnecting lines are plotted between those nodes (sites) which are visible to each other. At each location, where a cairn (or other notable monument) is present, this is used as the focal point. Where no cairn is present the cursus itself is used as the focal point.

We can observe a high degree of intervisibility for those cursus locations located in the east and southeast, and no intervisibility for cursus locations in the south and southwest. Most sites in the east and southeast exhibit a one-to-many visual relationship with other cursus locations and in many cases over considerable distances.

4.1 The Hill of Uisneach (1)

The Hill of Uisneach is an ancient ceremonial site in County Westmeath. It consists of numerous monuments and earthworks, prehistoric and medieval, including a megalithic tomb, barrows, enclosures, standing stones, holy wells and an ancient road. In Irish mythology Uisneach is deemed to be the symbolic and sacred centre of the island. It was said to be the burial place of mythical figures such as the god Lugh, and a place of assembly associated with the druids and the festival of Bealtaine.

The Hill of Uisneach does not have an associated cursus monument, however, the 'ancient road' is remarkably similar to the Hill of Tara linear earthwork known as 'Teach Midchuarta' - regarded by many as the remains of a cursus.

4.2 Keadeen Mountain (2)

Positioned on the western slopes of Keadeen Mountain, this long rectangular enclosure (L c. 286m; Wth c. 34m) is defined by banks and is aligned in a generally E-W direction on a steep slope, with a cairn (WI027-044----) just outside the NE corner.

The banks (avg. Wth 5m; H 0.3-0.6m) which are probably constructed from the mountainside scree, are almost entirely covered in heather and sod, but in one place the stone fabric of the bank is fully exposed. The lower end of the monument terminates with a bank while the upper end appears to be open. Keadeen Cairn is considered highly likely to contain a passage tomb.

4.3 Slievenamon Mountain (3)

Slievenamon (Irish: *Sliabh na mBan* - "mountain of the women") is a mountain with a height of 721 metres in County Tipperary. The mountain is steeped in folklore and is associated with the mythical hero Fionn mac Cumhaill. On its summit are the remains of ancient burial cairns.

The cursus is located on the E slope of Slievenamon. Cairn TS078-001---- is located 150m WNW of the cursus. Aligned roughly E-W, the monument, running downslope, consists of two roughly parallel stone banks overgrown with grass, moss and heather with an entire length of c. 360m. The banks are 90m apart, though they narrow to 75m at the upper western end. The upper end terminates with a bank on a relatively gentle slope, however, the ground level becomes increasingly steeper and both banks appear to terminate at the E end where the ground level becomes very steep.

4.4 Brewel Hill (4)

The Brewel Hill cursus is visible in aerial imagery as the cropmarks of two parallel fosses defining a long linear area. It is level at the SE end but rises gently towards the NW. A shallow linear hollow (D 0.3m; Wth 10m) while not particularly well-defined, is nonetheless traceable with an estimated length of circa 130m NW-SE, and terminates in a curved end at NW. From the NW, looking SE along its line, the cursus appears to be directed towards Keadeen Mountain.

At the summit of Brewel Hill there is a Ceremonial Enclosure or Henge type monument which contains the remnants of a stone circle known as the 'pipers stones'. Two of the boulders are granite, one is a red conglomerate ‚Äòpuddingstone‚Äô and one is of white quartz which is known locally as the ‚ÄòPiper‚Äôs Chair‚Äô.

4.5 Blackrock (Lugnagun Mountain) (5)

On the steep westernmost slope of Lugnagun ridge. A cairn (WI005-058---) is located on the summit of the ridge, at the eastern end of the monument.

This cursus consists of two parallel earthen banks (Wth c. 4.5m; H c. 0.5m) which are 36-39m apart, aligned roughly E-W, and visible over a distance of over 600m. At the upper, eastern end the cursus has an open terminal, with the southern bank stopping some distance short of the cairn, though this bank appears to have been robbed out. The downslope portion of both banks has been largely levelled but is visible on aerial photographs and Lidar imaging.

4.8 Newgrange (7)

Located within the *Br√∫ na B√≥inne* neolithic complex, this site comprises neolithic mounds, passage tombs, standing stones, henges and other prehistoric enclosures, some from as early as 35th century BC - 32nd century BC. The site thus predates the Egyptian pyramids and was built with sophistication and a knowledge of science and astronomy, which is most evident in the passage grave of Newgrange. The site is often referred to as the "Bend of the Boyne".

The Newgrange cursus is located approx 130m east from the Newgrange passage tomb. The monument consists of a raised U-shaped area running N-S, defined by scarps (L c. 100m, W c. 20m). The monument is highly visible on aerial photographs and lidar. Possibly originating from a pond at its northern end, the southern end terminates at the summit of the ridge and has commanding views to the south.

4.9 The Hill of Tara (8)

The Hill of Tara is an ancient ceremonial and burial site near Skryne in County Meath. According to tradition, it was the inauguration place and seat of the High Kings of Ireland, and it is also a significant site in Irish mythology. Tara consists of numerous monuments and earthworks ‚Äî from the Neolithic to the Iron Age‚Äîincluding a passage tomb (the "Mound of the Hostages"), burial mounds, round enclosures, a standing stone (believed to be the *Lia F√°il* or "Stone of Destiny"), and a linear earthwork believed to be a ceremonial avenue or cursus.

The linear earthwork is described as a sunken trackway running N-S, defined by large interrupted banks (W c. 10m, L c. 180m), connecting with less well-defined road system.

4.10 Ballyoskill (9)

This cursus is located on the steep W-facing slope of an N-S hill over a distance of c. 300m. The top of the monument is on the crest of the hill. It falls gently to W initially (for c. 11m), drops (H 0.35m) and flattens out slightly on a natural terrace (for c. 5m) before falling steeply down slope to the base of the hill. There are panoramic views S-W-N, with a very good view to the NE but only from the very top of the monument.

The feature consists of two low earthen banks (L c. 310m), running roughly parallel downslope. The cursus terminates at the crest of the hill with a curving terminal bank (Wth 3.6m; overall Wth 8m; int. H 0.9m; ext. H 0.13m). The banks appear to have a high concentration of stone, possibly stone-walling overgrown with grass sod. The stone is a local shale. The interior of the cursus is lower than the exterior. The monument widens as it runs down the slope. Cairn KK005-003001- is located at the northern summit of the hill.

4.11 Slievebawn Mountain (10)

This cursus is located on the the NW slope of Slievebawn. It consists of two parallel banks or grass/heather/turf-covered walls (Wth 2-2.6m; H 0.2-0.8m) composed of schist and quartz. The lower portion of the monument runs NW-SE for c. 160m and is 31m wide, while the upper portion changes direction slightly, running NNW-SSE for c. 113m and is slightly narrower, 22m wide.

The lower portion of the cursus is aligned with a circular cairn (CW020-016----) on the summit of the mountain. This cairn is c. 135m SE of the SE end of the monument. The upper portion of the cursus is aligned with a cairn (CW023-007----/WX008-001----) 3km to the SE on the summit of Mount Leinster, on the Carlow-Wexford border.

4.12 Coolasnaghta (Mount Leinster) (11)

On a steep mountain slope on the northern spur of Mount Leinster. It consists of two parallel banks (Wth c. 2m; H c. 0.4m), 33m apart, aligned NNW-SSE, which is visible over 400m. The southern portion of the cursus appears to be aligned with a cairn (CW023-007----/WX008-001----) 2km to the SSE on the summit of Mount Leinster, on the Carlow-Wexford border.

4.14 Knockainy West Site 1 (13) & Site 2 (14)

Knockainy West Site 1 (13)

Cursus monument on N side of Knockainy Hill. This cursus runs onto a group of five contiguous ring-barrows (LI032-139001-/004-) on the summit of Knockainy Hill. The cursus monument, stretching down the northern flank of the hill for a distance of approximately 750m, consists of two parallel banks, c. 9m in width and 0.5m in height, separated by a distance of 80m. There is no trace of a terminal at the south-west end of the cursus, where the bank fades out near the crest of the hill. This monument is likely to date from the Neolithic period and may be one of the earliest monuments on the hill. As one progresses up the cursus close to the summit of the hill, one looks directly to the south-west in the direction of the Paps of Anu.

Knockainy West Site 2 (14)

This possible cursus is described as following; 'This 80m-long rectangular enclosure consists of two parallel banks, each 10m in width and averaging 1m in height, separated by a distance of 25m. A bank forms a terminal at its northern end, while the southern end 'vanishes' over a steep incline.'

Astronomic Alignments & Modeling

**No Relationships Evident:** In the neolithic, when observed from the presumed focal points at Knockainy (Dunainy Mound LI032-139005- and Dun Cliach Ringfort LI032-131), there are no obvious rise/set relationships with notable neolithic sites. There is what can be described as a southmost crossquarter rise relationship with the highest peak in the Galtee Mountains where there are numerous cairns. For the purposes of this exercise, these sites are recorded as having no evident relationships with other cursus sites.

6. Interpretation and Deliberation

Deliberation

Using our cursus locations intervisibility network we demonstrate a high degree of intervisibilty for the eastern and southeastern cursus locations. I suggest and expect that neolithic peoples have been generally spatially aware of prominent locations on their skyline. Using computer modeling and astronomy calculations we demonstrate that many of these same intervisible cursus locations can be described as astronomically interrelational. That neolithic peoples were cognizant of the passing of time and placing specific importance on days such as the solstices is well understood and accepted. It is my contention that the number of demonstrable astronomic interrelationships evident between sites which have a cursus monument present must have been deliberately observed.

Spatial Contextual Awareness

In modern geographic parlance the concept I am seeking to demonstrate and support is termed spatial contextual awareness.

This hypothesis of spatial contextual awareness as it pertains to the ancient Irish might be described as follows:

The ancient Irish have been aware that by being present at particular locations at specific times, they will have observable and repeatable astronomic events (phenomena) visible at other prominent locations on the skyline. That the ancient Irish afforded special significance to these locations where they could observe, on divisional days within the solar and lunar cycles, the sun or moon rise from, or set into, what they regarded as sacred mountains or sacred spaces.

This concept will be further explored and demonstrated.
